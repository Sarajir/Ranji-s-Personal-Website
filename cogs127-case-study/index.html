Structured discovery for video replies on TikTok

Ran Ji · COGS 127 (Data-Driven UX / Product Design) · Spring 2026 · UC San Diego
Team: Nicholas Campos, Christian Lee, Chuck Davies, Ran Ji · TA section project

Short-form video apps already capture enormous attention. The design question we chased was narrower: when someone wants to stay inside one conversation thread, why is it still awkward to find and watch video replies?

Milestone 4 (May 10): first draft of Overview and User Research, plus our team’s high-fidelity Figma frames to ground the story visually. Later milestones add evaluation, metrics, and deeper reflection.

Overview

What we proposed

We explored an extension to TikTok (educational prototype; not affiliated with ByteDance) that adds a clearer path from a post’s comments into a dedicated, structured feed of video replies—including ways to scan multiple responses (for example, a grid) and to organize perspectives (for example, “general” vs. culture-specific buckets in our Figma set).

Problem

In interviews, heavy TikTok users described a familiar pattern: a video sparks curiosity, they open comments for context and reactions, and they sometimes want to keep watching responses that stay on-topic. Today, video replies can feel buried or inconsistent relative to the main recommendation feed, so the jump from “I’m interested in this thread” to “show me the reply videos” breaks flow.

Why I care (personal stake)

I’m sensitive to features that mainly optimize time-on-app. As a researcher, I’m interested in whether we can improve navigability and sense-making inside a thread without treating “more scrolling” as the only success metric. That tension—easier discovery versus healthier use—is something I’m carrying into how we frame outcomes later in the quarter.

My role

I contributed to research synthesis, interaction framing, and our high-fidelity prototype direction alongside my teammates. This write-up is my own framing of the work; our shared source of truth for raw notes lives in the team Google Doc.

User research

Goals & questions

We wanted to understand:

  • How people currently move from a video to related or response-based content.
  • Whether “staying on one topic” is something users want, or just something the algorithm sometimes delivers.
  • How comments and video replies fit into that mental model.
  • Whether a dedicated entry from the comment sheet into a reply feed felt understandable—and what organizational cues (e.g., popularity vs. newer vs. smaller creators) mattered.

Methods

We ran semi-structured interviews with TikTok users and used a short think-aloud-style prompt: open the app, scroll “normally,” and narrate what draws attention, when comments open, and when they hunt for more on the same topic. We also concept-tested our swipe / structured-reply idea with a verbal walkthrough (high-level, not a usability test of production UI).

What we heard (patterns)

  • Comments are a second layer of meaning. People use them for jokes, arguments, and context—not only text, but also signals of what’s “happening” around the post.
  • Topic-based exploration is real. Several participants described falling into “rabbit holes” when a topic clicks—but the path isn’t always reliable.
  • Friction when switching modalities. Moving from “watching” to “finding more videos about this exact thread” often means leaning on search, profiles, or luck in the For You feed.
  • Early enthusiasm for a structured reply surface. When we described a path from comments into a reply-only vertical feed, reactions were positive; participants said it could make continuation feel more intentional.

What surprised me

I expected comments to be secondary. Instead, they functioned almost like a map for social proof and narrative—especially when a post is controversial or meme-heavy. That pushed us to treat the comment sheet not as a dead-end panel, but as a legitimate on-ramp into deeper media.

I was also struck by how openly people connect “this would be useful” with “I’d probably spend more time in the app.” That honesty is useful for design—and it’s exactly why I want our later evaluation to include qualitative safeguards and clear task success, not only engagement counts.

How research shaped the design (Milestone 4 direction)

The Figma flow we built starts on the For You screen, opens comments, then enters a structured reply browsing mode (including grid-style scanning and category organization in later screens). The sequence is meant to mirror the mental model we heard: interest → social context → reply videos on the same topic.

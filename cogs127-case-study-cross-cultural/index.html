Cross-cultural sense-making inside one TikTok thread

Ran Ji · COGS 127 (Data-Driven UX / Product Design) · Spring 2026 · UC San Diego
Team: Nicholas Campos, Christian Lee, Chuck Davies, Ran Ji · Same Figma artifacts, sharper problem story

We’re not “adding a random video-reply button.” We’re asking: who struggles to interpret—and join—reply media when the post sits between cultures? This version of the case study centers cross-cultural viewers (e.g., international students calibrating U.S. TikTok discourse) and treats video in the comment thread as a first-class way to participate and compare reactions on the same parent video.

Note: This page is a second narrative for the same project so I can compare what reads stronger for TA review and my portfolio. Prototype PNGs are shared with the other case study page.

Overview

Problem statement

When someone tries to understand a viral TikTok—not only watch it, but see how people react—they lean on comments. For cross-cultural viewers (for example, Chinese international students in the U.S. who are still mapping local humor, slang, and norms), that need is sharper: they may want host-country reactions, and sometimes comparison with home-country takes on the same post.

But in our interviews, rich media in the thread—especially video responses tied to the comment context—felt hard to publish in-flow, hard to browse as a coherent set, and hard to interpret through an explicit “whose perspective is this?” lens. The For You feed optimizes engagement; it does not guarantee thread-scoped, interpretable sampling of communities’ reactions.

So we prototype a thread-scoped extension (educational; not affiliated with ByteDance): (1) a dependable path to upload and view video responses inside/near the comment context, (2) structured browsing of those replies (vertical feed + grid), and (3) culture / context lanes (e.g., General vs. Cultural in our Figma) so users can choose an interpretive filter instead of leaving it entirely to the algorithm.

Product claim, said carefully. TikTok’s feature set varies by market and version. We anchor claims in what our participants experienced as friction: even when partial reply-video patterns exist, cross-cultural sense-making inside one thread—publish → discover → compare—remained inconsistent for the journeys we studied.

Who we’re designing for (primary)

Primary lens: international students and other cross-cultural heavy users who already watch English TikTok but still feel they’re missing local nuance in replies—or who want to alternate between “what locals are saying” and “what my home context would say” on the same viral moment.

Goals

  • In-thread video participation: make posting and finding video responses in the comment context feel like a normal part of the thread—not a side quest.
  • Interpretable cultural browsing: let users intentionally sample replies under a chosen context lane (implementation-wise: self-tags, creator labels, language, or region—not pretend “nationality detection”).
  • Coherence over novelty: every screen serves one parent post’s ecosystem; we are not rebuilding the whole recommendation engine.

Non-goals

  • Replacing TikTok’s global recommendation system or solving full machine translation.
  • Definitive automated “culture classification” of people—lanes must stay transparent and user-controlled where possible.

User research

Goals & questions (re-centered)

  • How do cross-cultural viewers decide whether a reply “reads” local versus diaspora or international?
  • When do they want text versus video reactions—and what breaks when video replies are scattered?
  • Would explicit lanes (e.g., cultural filters) feel helpful—or reductive? What labels would feel fair?
  • Where would in-comment video capture reduce friction for people who won’t type a long English joke but still want to react?

Methods

Semi-structured interviews and think-aloud TikTok use; we concept-tested a comment→reply-video structure and culture-sorted browsing verbally. Sample size is small—this is directional, not proof of market demand.

Patterns (interpreted for this framing)

  • Comments = sense-making infrastructure. Especially for contentious or meme-heavy posts, comments teach readers the “rules of interpretation” for that thread.
  • Algorithm ≠ intentional comparison. Staying on one topic across communities is a different job than maximizing watch time.
  • Video replies raise the stakes. They carry tone and body language—but only help if users can find them and know who they’re hearing from.

What I’m still cautious about

Culture lanes can become stereotypes if implemented naïvely. Our prototype uses visual placeholders; a real ship would need transparent metadata, opt-in identity signals, and abuse reporting—I’ll fold that into later milestones.

Limitations. Exploratory interviews only; no moderated usability test on production-like builds yet. Next step is task-based testing with cross-cultural scenarios and tighter ethics copy for lanes.

Later milestones: participatory prototypes (e.g., lightweight generated stickers for non-native expression), evaluation metrics that privilege task success and comprehension, not only dwell time.
